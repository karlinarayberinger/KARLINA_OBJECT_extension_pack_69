When my feet are sore from taking a long walk (in properly fitting shoes), which parts of my foot are generating the sensation of aching and related warmth?

After a long walk in properly fitting shoes, the aching and warmth usually come from a mix of mechanically stressed tissues in the foot, not just one structure.

The main contributors are likely:

1. Plantar fascia and plantar soft tissues
The broad connective-tissue band along the sole, especially from the heel toward the arch, can ache after repetitive loading. The surrounding fat pads and connective tissue also get compressed thousands of times, creating a dull, warm, “worked” feeling.

2. Intrinsic foot muscles
Small muscles inside the foot help stabilize the arch and toes with every step. After a long walk, these can feel fatigued much like calf or thigh muscles do, especially in the arch, ball of the foot, and underside of the toes.

3. Tendons and tendon sheaths
Tendons from the calf and lower leg pass into the foot and help control push-off, arch support, and toe movement. Commonly stressed ones include the posterior tibial tendon along the inner ankle/arch, peroneal tendons along the outer foot, and toe flexor/extensor tendons. Irritated tendons can create aching, warmth, and sometimes a slightly “stringy” or localized sore feeling.

4. Joints and joint capsules
The many small joints of the foot, especially in the midfoot and forefoot, can ache from repetitive compression and motion. The metatarsophalangeal joints at the ball of the foot often generate soreness after lots of push-off.

5. Fat pads under the heel and ball of the foot
The heel pad and forefoot fat pad act like shock absorbers. After long walking, they can feel bruised, warm, or tender even without an actual injury.

6. Skin, tiny blood vessels, and superficial nerves
Warmth often comes from increased blood flow as the body delivers oxygen, clears waste products, and repairs minor tissue stress. The aching sensation is carried by small sensory nerve endings in muscles, fascia, ligaments, tendons, joint capsules, skin, and periosteum around bones.

7. Bone surfaces and periosteum, if the walk was especially long
Bones themselves are not usually what you “feel” directly, but the periosteum, the nerve-rich membrane around bone, can contribute to deep aching if the metatarsals, heel bone, or midfoot bones were repeatedly loaded.

So the simple version is: the ache is probably coming from fatigued foot muscles, compressed fat pads, stressed fascia/tendons/ligaments, and mildly irritated joint capsules, while the warmth is mostly increased blood flow plus mild inflammation from repetitive use.

That is usually normal after a long walk, especially if it fades with rest, food, hydration, elevation, gentle stretching, and sleep. More concerning signs would be sharp focal pain, swelling on one side, redness that spreads, numbness, inability to bear weight, pain that worsens over days, or a specific spot on a bone that hurts intensely when pressed.
